Smirates of the Smaribean Smea

The first time we were required to create a digital game for our studies, we used the software GameMaker. Despite having little prior experience with game programming, I was assigned the role of lead programmer—an exciting challenge that came with a steep learning curve.

Smirates of the Smaribean Smea is a game about a pirate crew stranded on a remote island after their ship crashes. You play as the captain, only to find your crew has vanished from the beach. Venture into the wild forest, explore hidden caves and ancient fortresses, and track down your missing crew. However, once found, they seem distant—no longer eager to follow your lead. It’s up to you to reunite your crew and find a way off the island.

Beyond being a fun little adventure game, Smirates also aims to teach players the value of teamwork and understanding different personalities. We were tasked with designing the game as a useful tool for first-year students. By embedding these lessons into gameplay, we hope to give newcomers a smoother and more engaging start to their academic journey.

Trailer showcasing Smirates of the Smaribean Smea (1)

Having practically zero coding experience before starting this project, I thought it was surprisingly easy to pick up. Granted, GML is not the most complex programming language to exist. However, I think I did an amazing job with the little time that we had to develop this game.

In the 2 months of development I coded more than 5500+ lines of GML code, skyrocketing my programming skills from a newbie to pretty experienced already.

This burst of experience helped me tremendously in projects developed after this.

User Interface elements (2)

Pause Interface (3)

One of the challenges I encountered while working on the game was designing the User Interface. I took this as an opportunity to develop two new skills simultaneously: 2D art and UI design.

I created the User Interface sprites (2) using Aseprite, a pixel art tool. With these newly made assets, I was able to assemble the actual interface, as shown in the pause screen (3), effectively strengthening both my 2D art and UI design abilities.

Another challenge was the marketing of our game. I decided to tackle this by making a trailer for our game (1) using Davinci Resolve (4). I used some music I made together with AI tools as the background track and went over some of the actions you can take in the game in a slightly comical manner. This was extremely helpful in getting eyes on our product and made the perfect finish to our development process.
